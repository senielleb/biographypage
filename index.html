My Life in Colors: A Journey of Emotional Growth

CHILDHOOD YEARS TEENAGE YEARS COLLEGE YEARS

Childhood Years

     Growing up, I was a very timid child.1 I didn’t have any encouragement to express what I truly felt as I always believe that showing vulnerability and emotions doesn't make you grow into an adult—a saying from the elders. I only see yellow and blue as it symbolizes the obvious representation of happiness and sadness.2 All I see is bright, vivid colors, but I don't have a clue on what those are.3 I am an only child, and my mother decided to work overseas without being able to see her for four years, leaving me with my father as my guardian.4 I always hang out with my cousins, and my wounds and bruises on my knees and elbows are the evidence that I always play outside since our one-bedroom apartment is not enough for me to run freely.5 They always label me as an 'iyakin' because I always cry.6 I am not throwing tantrums, but I was crying because my father always leaves me at my tito's and tita's house overnight because he's working.7 I am always confused that they always stop me from crying because it's annoying, as they said, it's a sign of weakness.8 They always mock me for showing a little bit of emotion of being sad.9 I am spoiled by my mother as she always gives me toys and gifts from overseas and as my father always bought me foods that I always wanted.10 I wasn't the energetic, aggressive, type of child; instead, I was quiet, observant, and imaginative.11 Since I had no siblings, I often entertained myself by requesting art materials and creating my world.12 Watching cartoons on our television was also one of my entertainments, but as I grew older, I found joy playing outside.13 My mother's gift, which is a scooter, became my bridge to socializing.14 I made friends and created special childhood memories.15 I do have countless memories that shape my entirety of my childhood because of my parents, relatives, cousins, and neighbors.16 But deep down, I am a very lonely child with a limited palette of color.17 Being an only child impacted me that much as I always consoled and dealt with my own issues and emotions.18 On a positive note, I learned how to easily adapt and change myself depending on the environment and the people around me. 19 In this age, I am still confused on how can I interpret my own colors.

Teenage Years

     As I entered adolescence, my world of simple colors became more vivid, it’s like a more chaotic blend of colors.1 I began to understand emotions beyond happiness and sadness.2 Red symbolized anger and frustration, white represented emptiness, and gray embodied dullness.3 My perspective on emotion expanded as I experienced the complexities of teenage life.4 This was the era where I began to shine, discovering my capabilities and talents–while also slowly realizing the harsh realities of the world.5 During this phase, I found a friend I believed would never leave me.6 I was also happy that I finally got to see my mother every year, though only for two months at a time.7 I anticipated her visits every year.8 Things went downhill during my high school years, but this was also when I experienced my peak happiness–the vibrant yellow moments of my life.9 Everything was so fresh for me that I was intrigued on what was gonna happen during my teenage years.10 Grade 7 was honestly my dumbest year, but I understood that it was my first time living as a teenage girl.11 My personality changed.12 From a quiet child, I transformed into someone with overflowing confidence.13 I joined a performing arts club where I showcase my talent.14 Performing helped build my self-esteem and gave me a reason to step out of my shell instead of hiding. 15 Academically, I excelled, but I struggled with my identity. 16I was repulsive and indecisive, constantly discovering new hobbies and interests.17 This resulted in me being a ‘jack of all trades but master of none’, leading to an uncertain identity crisis.18 I didn’t know what I was truly passionate about.19 Of course, I had my fair share of childish crushes, which I associate with a shade of pink–something immature and fleeting.20 At one point, I repressed all my emotions, mixing every color until they formed an unexplainable shade.21 My teenage years were challenging, as my emotions were constantly blending together, making them difficult to handle.22 Like most teenagers, I made dumb decisions and rebellious choices.23 Life turned dull and gray when the pandemic hit, as my personal development regressed.24 But I’m grateful that I eventually found my way back.25 Over time, I became immune to mixed emotions.26 I learned how to control them onto a canvas, depending on the situation.27 I realized that every theme in life needs a designated color, and that helped me navigate my emotions better.28 I finally learned the basic emotions that had once confused me as a child.

College Years

     Now, my color palette is more complicated but organized.1 My college experience was culture shock, as other people’s colors mixed with mine.2 I was scared because, for the first time, I met people who were too approachable, making me doubtful of their intentions.3 Academically, it was stressful because I chose my course out of practicality.4 From the start, I embraced the fact that it would be tough.5 I had moments of disappointment–whether my scores or my performance–but I didn’t dwell on them.6 I let myself feel it for a day and then moved on.7 I didn’t struggle with fitting in, but I felt let down when it came to building genuine connections.8 I'm not sure how I reached the realization, but I’ve learned that there’s no point in staying somewhere when there are no visible bright colors to hold on to.9 Growing up, I believed that each color represented a single emotion.10 But in college, I learned the complexity of human emotions.11 Colors aren’t singular; they can have multiple meanings.12 If I see red in person, I don’t immediately assume that they’re angry–it could mean they’re passionate, excited, or even in love. Similarly, I always associated white with isolation and emptiness, but I’ve realized that it can also represent being hopeful or at peace.13 Adjusting to this new environment was tough, especially since I wasn’t familiar with my course and had to navigate diverse personalities.14 I slightly regret not enjoying my high school years more, but college is now giving me a chance to fulfill my life and prepare for my future.15 I’m grateful that I found companions during the hard times, helping me forget the pressure surrounding us.16 I also met professional individuals that I am sure are helping us to build our future with their wisdom and knowledge.17 I have a few years to go to finish my college and I am open for opportunities and lessons even if it’s not pleasing.18 Now, I can see all the vivid colors in a positive light.19 I no longer keep my emotions bottled up, allowing them to flow freely.20 I can finally express myself.21 I can be sad, excited, annoyed, depressed, irritated, and still be able to smile, because they all make up who I am today.
